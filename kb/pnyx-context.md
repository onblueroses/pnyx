## What They Do

**Core services**:
- Political risk analysis for corporations and investors
- Strategic advisory for governments and international organizations

**Work product**: Reports, briefings, scenario analyses, advisory memos. Audience is boards, ministers, and senior executives - not the general public.

## Likely Problem Space

Analysts at a firm like Pnyx face these practical information challenges every day:

**Signal vs. noise at geopolitical speed**
Crises move fast. When a conflict escalates or an election is contested, information environments get flooded simultaneously with authentic reporting, disinformation campaigns, and gray-zone influence operations. Analysts need to distinguish these faster than they currently can.

**Source credibility at scale**
Their analysts monitor hundreds of sources across languages and jurisdictions. Assessing which outlets are independent vs. state-influenced, which analysts are credible vs. compromised - this is currently manual and slow.

**Narrative tracking**
Which narratives are gaining traction, in which countries, among which audiences? How do adversarial narratives spread? This is the kind of structural question their clients pay for.

**Briefing quality under time pressure**
Producing a high-quality briefing on a fast-moving situation requires synthesizing contested information quickly. An AI assistant that can surface what's confirmed vs. disputed vs. unknown would be directly useful.

**Scenario stress-testing**
Are the claims underlying a geopolitical scenario well-sourced? Can an AI flag which assumptions are disputed and which are solid?

## Implications for Hackathon

**Likely target user**: An Pnyx analyst or senior advisor, not a consumer or general journalist.

**Likely problem**: Something in the analyst workflow - monitoring, source assessment, narrative tracking, briefing support.

**What will resonate with Pnyx judges/advisors**:
- Tools that respect the complexity of geopolitical information (not binary fake/real)
- Sourcing transparency (where does a claim come from, who disputes it)
- Speed without sacrificing nuance
- European / multilingual angle - German, French, Russian, Chinese sources matter to them

**What will fall flat**:
- Consumer-facing apps
- Tools that only work on English-language social media
- "Misinformation detector" framing (too simplistic for this audience)
- Anything that feels like it's built for a US election context

**Strong framing for this audience**: "Intelligence-grade information quality, at analyst speed" - tools that let their team work more confidently with contested information.
